Functions & Divisions of the Nervous System
Sensory (afferent)
Receives stimuli from environment and body; transmits to CNS for interpretation
Motor (efferent)
Sends commands from CNS to muscles and glands; produces movement and secretion
Integration
CNS processes sensory input and coordinates appropriate motor responses
Regulation
Controls all body systems; maintains homeostasis via ANS
Cognition & memory
Higher functions: thought, learning, memory, language, personality (cerebrum)
Coordination
Cerebellum coordinates movement, balance, and muscle tone
Specialty: Neurology · Specialist: Neurologist (neur/o) · Neurosurgery · Psychiatry (mental/behavioral)  |  Key cf: neur/o (nerve), encephal/o (brain), cerebr/o (cerebrum), myel/o (spinal cord/marrow)
CNS vs. PNS
Central nervous system (CNS)
Brain (encephal/o) + Spinal cord (myel/o). Protected by skull (crani/o), vertebral column, and meninges (mening/o). Bathed in CSF.
Peripheral nervous system (PNS)
All nerves outside CNS: 12 pairs cranial nerves + 31 pairs spinal nerves. Subdivided into somatic (voluntary) and autonomic (involuntary) NS.
Autonomic NS (ANS) — involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, glands. Two divisions: Sympathetic ("fight or flight") and Parasympathetic ("rest and digest").
Key Terms at a Glance
Brain (encephal/o) Cerebrum (cerebr/o) Cerebellum (cerebell/o) Spinal cord (myel/o, cord/o) Nerve (neur/o) Meninges (mening/o) Skull (crani/o) Nerve root (radicul/o, rhiz/o) Dura mater (dur/o) CSF (cerebrospinal fluid)
Brain Structures — encephal/o
1
Cerebrumcerebr/o
Largest part of brain; divided into left and right hemispheres by longitudinal fissure. Four lobes: Frontal (personality, motor, executive function), Parietal (sensory integration, spatial awareness), Temporal (hearing, memory, language — Wernicke area), Occipital (vision). Corpus callosum connects hemispheres.
2
Cerebellumcerebell/o
"Little brain"; located posterior/inferior. Coordinates voluntary movements, balance, posture, and muscle tone. Damage → ataxia (uncoordinated movement).
3
Brainstem
Three parts: Midbrain (visual/auditory reflexes), Pons (breathing rhythm; relay station), Medulla oblongata (vital centers: heart rate, breathing, BP; 12th cranial nerve origin). Contains all ascending/descending tracts.
4
Meningesmening/o, meningi/o
Three protective membranes covering brain and spinal cord (PAD): Pia mater (innermost; on brain surface), Arachnoid mater (middle; web-like), Dura mater (dur/o — outermost; tough fibrous). Subarachnoid space contains CSF.
5
Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)
Clear fluid produced by choroid plexus in ventricles; cushions and nourishes brain and spinal cord; circulates in subarachnoid space. Normal: clear, colorless. Abnormal: cloudy (meningitis), bloody (hemorrhage). Assessed by lumbar puncture (LP).
6
Spinal Cordmyel/o, cord/o, chord/o
Extends from brainstem to L1–L2 vertebra (conus medullaris). 31 pairs of spinal nerves exit via nerve roots (radicul/o, rhiz/o). Grey matter (H-shaped; neuron cell bodies) surrounded by white matter (myelinated tracts). Cauda equina = nerve roots below conus.
Meninges Memory Aid — PAD
Pia mater
Innermost; "gentle mother"; adheres directly to brain/cord surface; highly vascular
Arachnoid mater
Middle; web-like appearance; subarachnoid space below contains CSF
Dura mater (dur/o)
Outermost; "tough mother"; thick fibrous; epidural space above it (site of epidural injections)
Protective Structures
Skull (crani/o) — 8 cranial bones; protects brain; craniotomy = surgical opening
Blood-brain barrier (BBB) — tight junctions in brain capillaries; selectively prevents entry of pathogens and many drugs
Vertebral column — 33 vertebrae protect spinal cord; nerve roots exit between vertebrae
Dermatomes — skin areas supplied by specific spinal nerve roots; used to locate lesion level
myel/o dual meaning: In this chapter = spinal cord (myelography, poliomyelitis). In Ch. 9 = bone marrow (osteomyelitis). Always use context to distinguish!
The Neuron — Structure & Signal Transmission
Neuron parts
Cell body (soma) — contains nucleus; metabolic center
Dendrites — receive signals FROM other neurons (like antennae)
Axon — single long projection; carries impulse AWAY from cell body
Myelin sheath — fatty insulation around axons; speeds conduction; produced by Schwann cells (PNS) and oligodendrocytes (CNS). Destroyed in MS.
Axon terminals — release neurotransmitters into synapse
Signal transmission
Synapse — gap between neurons where signal is transmitted
Neurotransmitter — chemical messenger released into synapse; binds receptor on next neuron. Examples: acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, GABA
Action potential — electrical impulse traveling along axon
Afferent neurons — carry signals TO brain (sensory)
Efferent neurons — carry signals FROM brain (motor)
Paresthesia (par- + esthesi/o + -ia) — abnormal sensation (tingling, numbness, "pins and needles"); from nerve compression or damage. Anosmia (an- + osm/o + -ia) — loss of smell; from COVID-19, head trauma, or nasal polyps.
Autonomic Nervous System — Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic
Sympathetic ("Fight or Flight")
Activated by stress; ↑ heart rate, ↑ BP, dilates pupils, bronchodilation, inhibits digestion, releases adrenaline (epinephrine/norepinephrine)
Parasympathetic ("Rest and Digest")
Dominant at rest; ↓ heart rate, promotes digestion (peristalsis), constricts pupils, promotes urination/defecation; neurotransmitter = acetylcholine
Synapse (syn- = together + -apse = join) = the junction between neurons where neurotransmitters are released.
Plegia & Paresis Terms
-plegia = complete paralysis
Hemiplegia (hemi-): paralysis of ONE side of body. Paraplegia (para-): paralysis of BOTH legs. Quadriplegia (quadri-): all 4 limbs. Monoplegia (mono-): one limb.
-paresis = weakness (incomplete)
Hemiparesis: weakness one side. Paraparesis: weakness of both legs. Quadriparesis: weakness all 4 limbs.
-plegia vs. -paresis: Plegia = COMPLETE loss of movement; Paresis = PARTIAL weakness. para- = beside/both; hemi- = half; quadri- = four; mono- = one.
Cerebrovascular accident (CVA) — Stroke
Sudden loss of brain function from interrupted blood supply. cerebr/o + vascul/o + -ar + accident. Ischemic (87%; clot blocks artery → FAST signs: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911) or Hemorrhagic (bleeding into brain). Treatment: tPA (thrombolytics) for ischemic if within 4.5 hrs.
Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
"Mini-stroke"; temporary (≤24 hrs, usually minutes) neurological deficit from brief vascular occlusion; no permanent damage; strong predictor of future CVA. trans- + ischem/o (hold back blood) + -ic. Requires urgent evaluation.
Encephalitis
Inflammation of the brain. encephal/o + -itis. Usually viral (herpes simplex most common; treat with acyclovir); can be bacterial or autoimmune. Symptoms: fever, headache, altered consciousness, seizures.
Meningitis
Inflammation of the meninges. mening/o + -itis. Viral (most common, usually benign) or bacterial (N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae — life-threatening). Classic triad: fever + headache + nuchal rigidity (stiff neck). Diagnosed by LP (CSF analysis).
Cerebral palsy (CP)
Non-progressive motor disability from brain damage during development (prenatal, perinatal, or early postnatal). cerebr/o + palsy (paralysis). Types: spastic (most common), athetoid, ataxic. Associated: intellectual disability, seizures, speech disorders.
Hemiplegia
Complete paralysis of one side of body. hemi- + -plegia. Most commonly from CVA affecting contralateral motor cortex.
Meningomyelocele
Protrusion of meninges AND spinal cord through a vertebral defect. mening/o + myel/o + -cele (hernia/protrusion). Severe form of spina bifida; causes paralysis below the defect; requires surgical repair at birth.
Poliomyelitis
Viral infection of spinal cord gray matter (anterior horn motor neurons). poli/o (gray) + myel/o + -itis. Can cause flaccid paralysis. Virtually eliminated by polio vaccine (IPV). Post-polio syndrome may develop decades later.
Quadriplegia / Paraplegia
Quadriplegia (quadri- + -plegia): paralysis of all 4 limbs; from cervical spinal cord injury. Paraplegia (para- + -plegia): paralysis of both legs; from thoracic/lumbar cord injury.
Paraparesis
Weakness (incomplete paralysis) of both lower extremities. para- + -paresis. From spinal cord compression, MS, or peripheral neuropathy.
Radiculopathy
Disease of nerve roots (radicul/o + -pathy). From disc herniation or spinal stenosis compressing nerve roots; causes radiating pain, numbness, weakness in distribution of affected nerve (e.g. sciatica = L4–S1 radiculopathy).
Paresthesia
Abnormal sensation: tingling, numbness, "pins and needles." par- + esthesi/o + -ia. From nerve compression (carpal tunnel, disc herniation) or systemic disease (MS, diabetes, B12 deficiency).
Multiple sclerosis (MS)
Autoimmune demyelination of CNS (myelin sheath destroyed). multi- + scler/o + -osis. White matter plaques form → varied neurological deficits (vision loss, weakness, numbness, fatigue, spasticity). Relapsing-remitting (most common). Diagnosed by MRI. Treated with DMTs (disease-modifying therapies).
Parkinson disease (PD)
Progressive degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra → dopamine deficit. Classic triad: TRAP = Tremor (resting pill-rolling), Rigidity, Akinesia (slowness), Postural instability. Shuffling gait, masked facies. Treated with levodopa/carbidopa.
Alzheimer disease (AD)
Most common dementia; progressive memory loss and cognitive decline. Beta-amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tangles. Acetylcholine deficiency. Gradual onset; age is primary risk factor. No cure; cholinesterase inhibitors slow progression.
Epilepsy / Seizures
Recurrent abnormal electrical discharges in brain. Types: focal (partial) vs. generalized. Absence seizures: brief staring spells (children). Tonic-clonic (grand mal): loss of consciousness + convulsions. Treated with antiepileptics (AEDs).
Narcolepsy
Chronic sleep disorder with excessive daytime sleepiness and sudden sleep attacks. narc/o (stupor/sleep) + -lepsy (seizure/attack). Associated with cataplexy (sudden muscle weakness triggered by emotion) from hypocretin/orexin deficiency.
Anosmia
Loss of sense of smell. an- + osm/o (smell) + -ia. Causes: COVID-19, head trauma, nasal polyps, Parkinson disease, Alzheimer disease (early sign).
Paresthesia
Abnormal sensations — tingling, numbness, burning, "pins and needles." par- + esthesi/o + -ia. Common in carpal tunnel, peripheral neuropathy, MS, radiculopathy.
Polysomnography (PSG)
Overnight sleep study. poly- + somn/o (sleep) + -graphy. Records brain waves (EEG), eye movements, muscle activity, heart rhythm, breathing, O₂ saturation. Diagnoses sleep apnea, narcolepsy, insomnia, REM disorders.
Hydrocephalus
Excess CSF in brain ventricles → increased intracranial pressure → brain damage. Congenital (spina bifida) or acquired (tumor, hemorrhage, meningitis). Treatment: ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt.
Diagnostic Procedures
Lumbar puncture (LP) — spinal tap
Needle inserted into subarachnoid space at L3–L4 to collect CSF; diagnoses meningitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage, MS, GBS; measures opening pressure
Electroencephalography (EEG)
electr/o + encephal/o + -graphy; records brain's electrical activity via scalp electrodes; diagnoses seizure disorders, encephalopathy, brain death
Myelography (myel/o + -graphy)
X-ray of spinal cord after contrast injected into subarachnoid space; evaluates disc herniation, spinal stenosis, tumors; largely replaced by MRI
Polysomnography (PSG)
poly- + somn/o + -graphy; comprehensive overnight sleep study; diagnoses sleep apnea, narcolepsy, movement disorders during sleep
MRI brain/spine
Best imaging for soft tissue (brain, spinal cord, MS plaques, tumors); detects strokes, demyelination, disc herniation, masses; no radiation
CT head
First-line for acute stroke (rules out hemorrhage before tPA); detects skull fractures, acute hemorrhage, hydrocephalus; fast and widely available
Nerve conduction study (NCS) / EMG
NCS measures electrical conduction along nerves; EMG records muscle electrical activity; diagnoses peripheral neuropathy, carpal tunnel, ALS, myopathies
Therapeutic Procedures
Surgical procedures
Craniotomy (crani/o + -tomy)
Surgical opening of the skull; to access brain for tumor removal, hematoma evacuation, aneurysm clipping, epilepsy surgery
Cordotomy (cord/o + -tomy)
Incision into spinal cord; interrupts pain pathways; treats intractable pain from cancer; cuts spinothalamic tract
Rhizotomy (rhiz/o + -tomy)
Cutting of a nerve root; treats chronic pain, spasticity (in CP); selective dorsal rhizotomy permanently reduces spasticity
Neurolysis (neur/o + -lysis)
Destruction or freeing of a nerve; treats chronic pain by destroying nerve fibers; can be chemical (phenol/alcohol) or surgical
Neurorrhaphy (neur/o + -rrhaphy)
Suturing of a severed nerve; microsurgical repair to restore nerve continuity after traumatic injury
Non-surgical
TENS — Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation
trans- + cutane/o + electr/o + neur/o; low-voltage electrical current applied through skin to interrupt pain signals; treats chronic and acute pain non-invasively
Pharmacology
Analgesics / Opioids Antiepileptics (AEDs) Dopaminergics (Parkinson) Antivirals (encephalitis) DMTs (multiple sclerosis) tPA (ischemic stroke) Cholinesterase inhibitors (AD)
